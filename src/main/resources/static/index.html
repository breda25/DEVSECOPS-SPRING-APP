On 28 February 2026, Israel and the United States launched surprise airstrikes on multiple sites and cities across Iran, killing Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei and numerous other Iranian officials. Iran responded with missile and drone strikes against Israel, US bases, and US-allied countries in the region.

After the Middle Eastern crisis began in 2023, Iran and Israel exchanged missile strikes in 2024, and Israel and the US launched airstrikes against Iran's nuclear facilities in the Twelve-Day War in June 2025. In January 2026, Iranian security forces killed thousands of protesters during the largest protests since the Islamic Revolution. US president Donald Trump threatened to take military action against the Iranian regime for the killings, as part of President Trump's desire to change the regime. Iran and the US held indirect nuclear negotiations in February, which saw substantial progress, according to the mediator. Meanwhile, the US undertook its largest military buildup in the Middle East since the US-led 2003 invasion of Iraq.

The joint US and Israeli attack assassinated Ali Khamenei, Iran's supreme leader, whose compound was destroyed. Other Iranian officials were also killed. Iran then launched hundreds of drones and ballistic missiles at targets in Israel and at US military bases in Bahrain, Jordan, Kuwait, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, Turkey, and the United Arab Emirates. There were civilian casualties in Iran, as well as damage to schools, hospitals, and cultural heritage sites. Civilian targets in Azerbaijan, Kurdistan, and Oman were targeted by Iran. Britain's Akrotiri military base on Cyprus was struck by a drone. The conflict between Hezbollah and Israel, which since 2024 had been limited to Israeli strikes on Hezbollah in Lebanon, escalated into the 2026 Lebanon war when Hezbollah resumed missile and drone fire at Israeli sites, and Israel greatly expanded its airstrikes in Lebanon and resumed the bombing of Hezbollah targets.

The conflict led to immediate surges in oil and gas prices, widespread disruptions in aviation and tourism, and heightened volatility in financial markets. Iran forced the closure of the Strait of Hormuz and attacked energy facilities, disrupting global oil and gas shipments.

Background

United States relations with Iran

A US and UK-backed coup d'état in 1953 deposed Iranian prime minister Mohammad Mosaddegh and strengthened the rule of the Shah, Mohammad Reza Pahlavi. Resentment of the Shah's deference to Western interests led to the 1979 revolution in which he was overthrown, after which Iran became an Islamic republic, severed diplomatic ties with the United States and Israel, and held the American embassy staff hostage for over a year.

Tensions between Iran and the US and Israel further escalated following the October 7 attacks on Israel and the start of the Gaza war in 2023, during which Israel weakened Iranian-backed militias across the Middle East. Iran and Israel kept engaging in rhetoric in the months to come until Israel waged a 12-day war on Iran in June 2025 that included an American airstrike aimed at destroying Iran's nuclear facilities.

Iran nuclear issue

The United States withdrawal from the JCPOA in 2018 led to the re-imposition of sanctions on Iran. The second Trump administration reimposed the "maximum pressure" approach on Iran. Secretary of the Treasury Scott Bessent labelled the collapse of the Iranian currency in December 2025 as the "grand culmination" of this strategy.

At the State of the Union Address in late February 2026, President Trump stated that Iran had restarted its nuclear program and was developing missiles capable of striking the US. Days later, the IAEA discovered that Iran had hidden highly enriched uranium in an underground facility.

Prelude

Beginning in late December 2025, massive nationwide anti-government protests erupted in Iran. Originally driven by discontent over the economic crisis, they escalated into explicit calls for the overthrow of the Islamic Republic government. On 2 January 2026, Trump threatened a "lock and loaded" military intervention in Iran if the government decided to kill peaceful protesters, and later announced that a US "armada" was heading to the Middle East.

Just before the strikes began, on 27 February 2026, Oman's Foreign Minister said a "breakthrough" had been reached in nuclear negotiations and Iran had agreed both to never stockpile enriched uranium and to full verification by the IAEA. However, US Middle East envoy Steve Witkoff said Iran had begun recent nuclear talks by insisting on its "inalienable right" to enrich uranium.

Hostilities

First week (28 February – 6 March)

On 27 February at 3:38 p.m. EST, Donald Trump gave the order to proceed with Operation Epic Fury. US missiles, drones, and Israeli fighter jets began striking Iran the next day, around 9:45 a.m. IRST. The operation was codenamed Operation Roaring Lion by Israel. The Israeli Air Force (IAF) said that it had struck 500 military targets in western and central Iran, including air defenses and missile launchers, using about 200 fighter jets, in the largest combat sortie in its history.

The strikes were coupled with cyberattacks on Iranian infrastructure, media, and phone apps, including messages calling on Iranians to rise up against their government. The cyberattacks resulted in a near-total internet blackout in Iran, lasting over 60 hours.

Iranian forces reacted within hours by launching missiles in an operation it named Operation True Promise IV. Iran struck Israeli targets in Tel Aviv and Haifa as well as multiple countries throughout the Persian Gulf region. Attacks were reported across the Gulf States. Major targets included Bahrain's capital Manama, Kuwait International Airport, the United Arab Emirates' capital Abu Dhabi, Riyadh, and Erbil International Airport.

On 4 March, an Iranian Navy frigate, IRIS Dena, was sunk in the Indian Ocean by United States Navy submarine USS Charlotte, about 40 nautical miles south of Galle, Sri Lanka. The IDF also announced that an F-35I "Adir" shot down a Russian-made Iranian Yak-130 fighter jet over Tehran, the first time that an F-35 has ever shot down a manned fighter jet in air-to-air combat.

Second week (7–13 March)

On 7 March, the US sent a third aircraft carrier, USS George H. W. Bush, to the Middle East. On 8 March, Khamenei's second son, Mojtaba, was designated as Iran's new supreme leader.

Israeli airstrikes hit some of Iran's oil storage facilities, killing four people. Strikes on fuel depots near Tehran caused a "river of fire" to pour out along the streets in the surrounding area, and the city became engulfed in a cloud of thick black smoke, causing toxic acidic black rain to fall in the surrounding area.

Third week (14 March – present)

The US conducted a large bombing raid on Kharg Island, Iran's oil export hub. Military forces were targeted while the oil infrastructure was not targeted "for reasons of decency" according to Trump. Early on 15 March, heavy airstrikes were reported in the Isfahan area, along with explosions near Shiraz and southern Tehran. Iran's military vowed to "pursue and kill" Israeli prime minister Benjamin Netanyahu.

Strikes and casualties

On 28 February 2026, Ali Khamenei, the supreme leader of Iran, was assassinated. Early on 1 March, Iranian state media announced that Khamenei had been killed in the strikes on his compound.

Iranian defense minister Aziz Nasirzadeh and IRGC commander Mohammad Pakpour were killed by Israeli airstrikes. The IDF later stated that it had confirmed the deaths of seven Iranian security leaders.

Terminal High Altitude Area Defense (THAAD): Formerly Theater High Altitude Area Defense, is an American anti-ballistic missile defense system designed to intercept short-, medium-, and intermediate-range ballistic missiles in their terminal phase. During the conflict, one THAAD radar was reported destroyed or seriously damaged.

Impact

Economic and geopolitical impact

The war has global economic consequences: The conflict led to immediate surges in oil and gas prices, widespread disruptions in aviation and tourism, and heightened volatility in financial markets. Analysts projected potential global inflation increases and risks of recession if disruptions persisted, particularly through closures of key shipping routes like the Strait of Hormuz and strikes on gas and oil facilities.

Oil prices rose following a reduction in traffic through the strait, and strikes on oil facilities in countries such as Saudi Arabia and the UAE. Oil prices went over $100 per barrel to $114 per barrel, giving it the highest prices since the COVID-19 pandemic.
